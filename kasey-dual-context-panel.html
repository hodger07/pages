The Dual-Context Question

Should kaseyhodge.com explicitly serve both professional and romantic intros — or should it stay context-neutral and let the sender carry the framing?

Option A: Context-neutral. The page shows who Kasey is. Same page, different context, the sender carries the framing.

Option B: Serve both explicitly. The page signals it works for both — "whole person" framing that reads professionally AND personally.

Option C: Something else the agents come up with.

Opus Synthesis

Verdict
Option A — Context-Neutral. Unanimous.

Nine agents. Zero dissent on the core question. The page should NOT explicitly acknowledge it serves dual purposes. The consensus wasn't just "A is better than B" — it was "B would actively damage what the page already does well." The current page works in both contexts because it doesn't try to. That's the insight.

The warm-intro model depends on a clean separation of roles: the sender provides context ("you two should meet" vs. "he'd be great for the project"), and the page provides evidence. The moment the page starts doing the sender's job — by acknowledging it serves romantic and professional purposes — it undermines the sender's credibility and triggers the viewer's sales-detection radar. A page that says "I work for both" is a page that works for neither.

What This Means for the Page

What stays: Everything. The structure, the photos, the tone, the "I don't narrate things" closing, the design language. The page's restraint is its most powerful feature. The warm linen background, the serif-sans pairing, the casual-but-confident photo choices — all of it splits the professional/personal line without trying to. Don't restructure, don't add sections, don't create a "personal" area.

What changes — one surgical edit: The "How I'm wired" section is the only place the page feels authored rather than lived. "I'd rather build something than talk about building something. I'd rather be underestimated than over-positioned." These are correct theses, but they read like theses. Replace one of these with a specific moment — a Tuesday, a story, something concrete that shows the principle instead of stating it. The rest of the page does this beautifully (the night runs, Blu on the jet, the "work over school" choice). "How I'm wired" needs to match that register. This makes the section warmer and more human without changing the strategy at all — and it removes the one moment where a dating-context viewer might feel like they're reading a founder's bio.

What gets added or removed: Nothing. The page's power is in its economy. Every section earns its place. There's no missing ingredient — just one section that needs to trade a principle for a story.

The one-liner for Kasey: "Your page isn't a pitch for two audiences — it's a window into one person. The sender does the framing. The page just has to be you."
