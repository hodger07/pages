Kasey Hodge

Houston, TX

Late twenties. Houston born and raised. I build things—businesses, mostly, but also the kind of life where a Tuesday might involve a job site in DFW and a night run back home. I like working with my hands, working with my family, and figuring out hard problems with good people.

I co-built our family’s HVAC and electrical business across the Houston and Dallas–Fort Worth markets. No MBA. No venture capital. Just showing up, learning the trade, and scaling something real. The kind of work where the results are tangible—buildings that stay cool, lights that turn on, people who get hired.

Most of my best thinking happens after dark, on foot.

What I care about

Blu, my lab. She flies better than most passengers I know.

Running—usually at night, usually alone, usually when I need to think. Aviation, because it teaches you to respect systems and stay calm when things get interesting. Family, because everything I’ve built started there.

I keep a pretty small circle and a pretty private life. Not because there’s a strategy behind it—I just don’t feel the need to narrate things as they happen. If you Googled me and ended up here, that’s probably all you need to know about how I use the internet.

Blu, stealing the better seat. As usual.

How I’m wired

I’d rather build something than talk about building something. I’d rather be underestimated than over-positioned. Most of the people I admire are the ones you’ve never heard of—they’re just quietly good at what they do, and generous with the people around them.

I got here by choosing work over school, which isn’t the path people expect. But the tradeoff was getting ten years of real experience while everyone else was getting a degree. I don’t think either way is right. Mine just fit.

People ask me sometimes what the plan is. Build bigger? Go public? Move to a coast? I don’t really think like that. I think about whether the people around me are good, whether the work is honest, and whether I’m better this year than last year. The rest sorts itself out.

My dad taught me that the way you do anything is the way you do everything. I haven’t found a reason to argue with that yet.

The goal was never to be impressive. The goal was to be solid.
